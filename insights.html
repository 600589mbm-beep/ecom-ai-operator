Ecommerce conversion benchmarks — who converts best, and why

What "good" looks like by retailer and category in 2026 — and the listing, ad, email, and support levers that move your own rate toward it.

By retailer

The leaders aren't winning on luck

Each leader engineered friction out of the buy. That's a playbook you can copy at your scale.

🟧 Amazon — the conversion king

Standard shoppers convert in the ~10–15% range, while Prime members are widely cited as converting north of 70% once they reach a product page. The driver isn't magic — it's zero-friction checkout, saved payment details, and deep consumer trust. Takeaway: remove every extra click and reassurance gap between "interested" and "paid."

🐾 Chewy — ~6.0–6.5%

Chewy dominates pet care by leaning into consumable, subscription buying (Autoship). Customers need recurring shipments of the same items, so repeat-purchase conversion and lifetime value are enormous. Takeaway: if any product is replenishable, build a subscribe-and-save path and an email flow that reminds at the right cadence.

🔵 Best Buy — ~3.0–3.5%

In higher-ticket tech, Best Buy blends online browsing with local inventory and in-store pickup, plus highly structured, technical spec sheets. Immediate gratification + clarity beats friction. Takeaway: for considered purchases, win on specification clarity and fulfillment options, not discounts.

By category

Some sectors convert higher by nature

Lower purchase friction and frequent replacement lift conversion. Higher-AOV, considered categories sit lower — and that's normal.

High converters
  • Food & Beverage / Groceries — ~4.9–5.5%
  • Arts & Crafts / Hobbies — ~5.1–5.2%
  • Personal Care & Beauty — ~4.3–6.8%
Naturally lower
  • Luxury fashion — often below 1.5%
  • Heavy furniture — often below 1.5%
  • Other high-AOV, considered buys

Figures are industry benchmarks, not guarantees — your category, traffic source, and price point all shift the baseline. Use them to judge your own rate against the right peer set, not against Amazon.
